**Overview**
>This project will look deeper into the relationship between housing affordability and environmental health risks across neighborhoods in the United States of America. The analysis will be rooted in two public datasets found on Data.gov. The first dataset is the Housing Affordability Data System, also known as HADS, which is derived from the American Housing Survey (AHS). This dataset gives measures of housing affordability, income categories in relation to federal housing thresholds, and housing cost burdens for renters and home owners. The second dataset is the Environmental Health Hazard Exposure Index, which holds information for potential exposure to harmful environmental toxins across the States on a neighborhood level. This dataset essentially summarizes environmental health risks by combining the standardized measurements for carcinogenic, respiratory, and neurological air pollution hazards from the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) into a single combined index score.
>The goal of our project is to determine whether communities with higher housing cost burdens or lower income levels are exposed to greater environmental health risks. We will also examine how they might differentiate across regions in the United States. We will combine these datasets by using shared geographic identifiers, which we will probably have to implement ourselves to look into potential patterns across all regions. This project will follow the data lifecycle approach by following the planning, data acquisition, storage and organization, integration, data cleaning, analysis, and documentation steps. Our final project will have a reproducible workflow that allows another user to recreate our complete process.

**Research or Business Questions**
>The primary focus of this project will be understanding how housing affordability and environmental health risks intersect across geographic areas in the United States. The main research question looks into whether neighborhoods with higher housing cost burdens also experience a greater exposure to environmental health hazards. This research question was sparked by overall interest in the current housing market combined with severe weather warnings in the Champaign-Urbana area. These three questions will guide how we integrate the datasets as we begin our initial analysis. By the end of this project, we should have a deliverable that clearly examines potential relationships between economic housing pressures and environmental conditions through the usage of statistics and data analysis.

**Datasets**
>The first dataset in this project is the Housing Affordability Data System. HADS is a collection of data files from the American Housing Survey through 1985 to present and from the Metro American Housing Survey from 2002 to present. HADS categorizes housing units by affordability and households by income in relation to multiple federal housing benchmarks. These benchmarks include an adjusted median income, fair market rent, and poverty income thresholds. This dataset also includes a measure of housing cost burden. Housing cost burden is described by the proportion of household income spent on housing expenses for renters and homeowners. These measures are popular measures when used in housing policy analysis that has served as a basis for the U.S. Department of Housing tables since 2001. We will use HADS for their consistent set of affordability measures that can be applied to different years and geographic areas. HADS is only available as a public use file before 2013. It has become an internal use file in 2015. We will focus on the 2013 public use dataset. 
>The second dataset is the Environmental Health Hazard Exposure Index. This index summarizes potential exposure to harmful environmental toxins at the neighborhood level. This index is a linear combination of standardized estimates from the Environmental Protection Agency for air quality hazards. It combines measures of carcinogenic, respiratory, and neurological hazards that are associated with environmental pollution. These estimates are standardized and aggregated to produce a single index value to represent relative environmental health risk within a given geographic area. In our project, this dataset will be our indicator of environmental health risk exposure that can be compared with housing affordability measures from our first dataset.
